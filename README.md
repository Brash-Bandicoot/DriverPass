# DriverPass System Analysis & Design Portfolio Artifact

## Repository Contents
This repository contains two portfolio artifacts developed for the DriverPass project:

- **Project One:** Business Requirements Document  
- **Project Two:** System Design Document  

Together, these documents demonstrate my ability to gather customer requirements, translate them into system needs, and design a solution that meets both business and user expectations.

---

## Project Summary (DriverPass)
DriverPass was the client for this project. DriverPass is a company focused on improving student success when preparing for driving exams by offering a structured training experience instead of relying only on memorizing practice tests. The system I designed supports a platform that helps students learn driving rules and skills through online tools and scheduling, while also supporting staff operations such as managing customer accounts, lesson scheduling, and progress tracking.

---

## What I Did Particularly Well
One area I feel I did particularly well was translating the client’s goals into clear, organized requirements and ensuring those requirements mapped into the system design. I focused on keeping the documentation structured and readable, and I made sure system features (like scheduling and account management) were supported by specific requirements and constraints. I also worked to balance technical accuracy with explanations that a nontechnical stakeholder could understand.

---

## What I Would Revise and How I Would Improve It
If I could revise one part of my work, I would strengthen the traceability between requirements and specific design components. While the system design reflects the requirements, I would improve the documentation by adding clearer requirement-to-feature mapping (for example, a traceability matrix or more explicit references from design sections back to requirement IDs). This would make it easier for a stakeholder or developer to verify that every requirement is fully implemented and to identify where changes would impact the design.

---

## Interpreting User Needs and Implementing Them Into the Design
I interpreted the user’s needs by identifying who the users were (students, instructors, and administrators) and focusing on what each group must accomplish in the system. That interpretation guided the design choices—for example, ensuring students can easily access training resources and schedule lessons, while administrators can manage accounts, schedules, and business rules. Considering the user’s needs is critical because a system can meet technical requirements and still fail if the workflow is confusing, inefficient, or does not match what users actually need to do. User-centered thinking improves adoption, usability, and overall success of the system.

---

## How I Approach Software Design (Now and Going Forward)
My approach to designing software starts with understanding the problem and clearly defining requirements before jumping into implementation details. I prefer to break down the system by identifying users, use cases, constraints, and key business processes first, and then translate those into models and design decisions. In the future, I will continue using strategies such as:
- **Requirements elicitation and validation** (ensuring the “what” is correct before building the “how”)
- **Use case modeling** to confirm user goals and workflows
- **Iterative refinement** of diagrams and documentation so the design stays aligned with requirements
- **Risk and constraint awareness** (security, access control, reliability, and maintainability)
- **Traceability practices** to ensure requirements are consistently reflected in the final design

This process helps ensure the final solution is both technically sound and aligned with the customer’s real needs.
